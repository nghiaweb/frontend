Gemstones Sapphire

Hard, tough & beautiful

Sapphire, a member of the Corundum family, is known to be hard, tough and beautiful. In normal every day wear this variety is favored in all types of jewelry.

Sapphire is chemically aluminum oxide yet it often contains other elements that give it different colors. The range of colors is consistent with the rainbow, and Sapphire offers not only many colors but vibrant and rich colors. When colored by the element Chromium, the Corundum is red. In this case we call it a Ruby rather than Sapphire yet they are both Corundum. Sapphires that are colored with the elements Titanium and Iron are blue. The color blue Sapphires are in the highest demand around the world. The most desirable color of blue is known as cornflower blue, a saturated and bright blue. Other colors in high demand besides blue are known as fancy Sapphires. The most expensive fancy color is a pinkish orange known as Padparadcha, named after a Japanese water lily. Other popular colors include yellow, green, purple, pink, gray and black.

While the most well-known Sapphires are faceted gems, cabochons have been enjoyed thousands of years longer. A phenomenon of light in the form of a floating six ray star is very desirable in a Sapphire cabochon. These stars can be naturally occurring due to the hexagonal shape of the crystal structure aligning Rutile inclusions in the six ray pattern. Man can also create a six ray star in Corundum by diffusing the star into the surface. Both natural and man-made stars will float across the surface when turned in the light. Rare fixed, non-floating stars known as Trapiche stars are also seen in Corundum and are collector's gemstones.

Due to the fact that Sapphires are extremely tough, coupled with a very high consumer demand for beautiful Sapphire gems, many treatments are used to enhance the appearance of less marketable gems. The most common and accepted treatment is traditional heat and very little value is lost with this enhancement compared to the large increase in value achieved by this ancient practice. Rarely a Sapphire will be beautiful without any heating and these do command a much higher price. Treatments such as diffusion and glass filling should be avoided for long term purposes.

Sapphires that are only traditionally heated or are unheated will last for perhaps millions of years with no sacrifice of beauty. Sapphires of one carat weight and up will command higher prices than those under one carat. Prices will climb incrementally when reaching whole numbers with a more significant jump at five carats. A fall in carat price is seen at higher weights, especially at 100 carats as the usefulness of the gem declines due to the overly large size. Moderate and useful sizes are the most desirable.
